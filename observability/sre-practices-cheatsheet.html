01SLI vs SLO vs SLA

The single most-asked SRE concept. Nail the precise definitions and the relationship between the three.

SLI — Indicator

A quantitative measure of some aspect of the service, expressed as a ratio of good events to valid events.

SLI = good events / valid events × 100%

e.g. proportion of HTTP requests served in < 300 ms; proportion returning non-5xx.

SLO — Objective

A target value or range for an SLI over a time window. The internal reliability goal you engineer toward.

SLO: 99.9% of requests
     < 300ms
     over 28 days

Set by the team; stricter than the SLA.

SLA — Agreement

A contract with the customer that includes consequences (credits, penalties) if objectives are missed.

SLA: 99.5% monthly
     uptime, else
     10% bill credit

A business/legal artifact, not an engineering one.

Mental model SLI is the measurement, SLO is the goal, SLA is the promise. Set the internal SLO tighter than the external SLA so you get paged and fix things before you breach the contract and owe money. Rule of thumb: SLO ≥ SLA + one nine of headroom, or at least a comfortable margin.
The three at a glance
AspectSLISLOSLA
What it isA metricA target on that metricA contract

Consequence of missNone (it's just data)Spend error budget; freeze releasesFinancial penalty / credits

⚠ Trap "Do you always need an SLA?" — No. Not every service faces external customers. But every service worth running should have SLOs and SLIs. Also: 100% is the wrong reliability target for basically everything — it is impossible, infinitely expensive, and the marginal nine is invisible to users behind their own ISP/device unreliability. The right target is "reliable enough" for the user, no more.

02Choosing good SLIs

An SLI should reflect what the user actually experiences, not internal implementation trivia. CPU utilization is not an SLI; "did the user's request succeed quickly" is. Google groups SLIs by request category:

SLI types by service category (Google SRE Workbook)
CategoryRelevant SLIsGood-event definition
Request/response (user-facing)Availability, Latency, QualityNon-5xx response, served under threshold
Data processing / pipelinesFreshness, Correctness, Coverage, ThroughputRecords processed correctly & recently
StorageDurability, Latency, AvailabilityData readable, not lost, returned fast

Request-based vs window-based SLIs

Request-based

Ratio computed over individual events.

good_requests / valid_requests

Directly maps to the error budget. Preferred for burn-rate alerting. A single 10-min outage of a low-traffic hour dilutes into the whole month's request count.

Window-based

Ratio of good time-windows to total windows (e.g. "was each 1-min bucket good?").

good_minutes / total_minutes

Closer to how SLAs word uptime ("99.9% of minutes"). A window is "bad" if its error rate exceeds a sub-threshold. Better when traffic is bursty/low.

Good-SLI checklist
  • User-centric — measured as close to the user as feasible (at the load balancer, not deep inside a microservice).
  • Predictive of happiness — moves in the same direction as user satisfaction.
  • Few — pick a small number (availability + latency covers most services). Too many SLOs = none are respected.
  • Has a clear "valid event" denominator — decide up front how health checks, bots, and 4xx client errors are counted.
⚠ Trap Averages hide pain. Never define a latency SLO on the mean — a p50 of 100 ms can hide a p99 of 8 s that furious users feel. Define latency SLIs on percentiles (p95/p99) and as a threshold count ("% of requests under 300 ms"), which is what burn-rate math needs.

03Error budgets & the math

The error budget is the bridge between reliability and velocity. This is the section interviewers probe hardest — know the downtime numbers cold.

Definition

If your SLO is X%, your error budget = 100% − X% — the allowed amount of unreliability over the window. It is a budget to be spent, deliberately, on risky launches, experiments, and planned maintenance. As long as budget remains, developers ship freely; when it is exhausted, the policy kicks in.

How to derive it live

Interviewers love "compute 99.9% monthly downtime on the spot." Do it as: 30 days = 43,200 min → 0.1% = 43,200 × 0.001 = 43.2 min = 43m 12s. Each extra nine divides downtime by 10. So 99.99% monthly ≈ 4.32 min, 99.999% ≈ 25.9 s.

Error budget policy

The policy is a pre-agreed, written contract between SRE, dev, and product that says what happens as budget depletes. It must be signed off before an incident so it isn't renegotiated under pressure.

A typical tiered error-budget policy
Budget remainingPostureActions
> 50%HealthyShip freely; take reliability risks; run experiments & chaos tests.
10–50%CautionPrioritize reliability work; slow risky launches; review recent regressions.
Exhausted (0%)FreezeFeature freeze. Only reliability fixes and P0 security merge. Ships resume when budget recovers over the trailing window.
Freeze-on-exhaustion — the core idea

When the budget is spent, the release valve closes automatically. This depersonalizes the "ship vs. stabilize" fight: it's not SRE blocking devs, it's the data. The freeze creates a self-correcting loop — teams that burn budget on bad launches lose their ability to launch until reliability is restored, which realigns incentives around quality. Escalation paths (e.g. VP sign-off) exist for genuine emergencies.

⚠ Trap "We had zero incidents this quarter, isn't that great?" — Not necessarily. Consistently not spending the error budget means the SLO is too strict / you're over-investing in reliability at the cost of feature velocity. Unspent budget is a signal to loosen the SLO or ship faster / take more risk. The goal is to spend the budget, not hoard it.

04Burn-rate alerting

The modern, correct way to alert on SLOs. Expect to be asked why multi-window multi-burn-rate beats a naive threshold.

Burn rate defined

Burn rate = how fast you are consuming the error budget relative to the "even" pace. A burn rate of 1 spends the entire budget exactly over the SLO window (e.g. 30 days) — right on target. A burn rate of 10 spends it 10× faster; at that pace a 30-day budget is gone in 3 days.

Burn rate ↔ time-to-exhaustion (for a 30-day / 99.9% SLO)
Burn rateBudget consumed in 1 hourFull budget exhausted inSeverity
1×~0.14%30 dayson target
6×~0.83%5 daysslow burn — ticket
14.4×2%~2 daysfast burn — page
36×5%~20 hoursvery fast — page
1000×100% (in that hour)~43 mintotal outage

Why multi-window, multi-burn-rate?

Google's SRE Workbook recommends combining several alerts at different burn rates and window lengths. Each alert requires both a long window (the trigger) and a short window (a confirmation the problem is still happening right now), which kills the two failure modes of single alerts:

  • Slow single threshold → poor detection time: a small-but-sustained error rate takes ages to trip.
  • Fast single threshold → poor reset time / false pages: a 1-minute blip pages you, and it keeps firing after the incident resolved.
Canonical Google multi-window / multi-burn-rate config (99.9% SLO)

Page (fast)14.4×1 hour5 min2%
Page (medium)6×6 hours30 min5%
Ticket (slow)3×1 day2 hours10%
Ticket (slow)1×3 days6 hours10%

Short window ≈ 1/12 of long window. The short window must also be over threshold for the alert to fire — this gives fast reset when the incident clears.

⚠ Trap "Why not just alert when availability drops below 99.9%?" — because that alerts on the SLO threshold, not the burn rate. It fires after you've already breached, gives no early warning, and doesn't distinguish a catastrophic outage (page now!) from a slow leak (open a ticket). Burn-rate alerting is symptom-based and budget-aware: it pages proportionally to how fast you're heading toward breach.

05The four golden signals

From the Google SRE book: if you can only measure four things about a user-facing system, measure these.

① Latency

Time to service a request. Separate successful from failed latency — a fast 500 error can otherwise make latency look great. Track distributions (p50/p95/p99), not averages.

② Traffic

Demand on the system — requests/sec, transactions/sec, concurrent sessions, I/O rate. The denominator that gives errors and latency context.

③ Errors

Rate of failed requests — explicit (5xx), implicit (200 with wrong content), or policy (too slow counts as failed). Directly feeds the error budget.

④ Saturation

How "full" the system is — CPU, memory, disk I/O, queue depth. Utilization vs. capacity; the leading indicator of imminent latency/error degradation. Watch the most constrained resource.

Related mnemonics (know these too)
  • USE (Brendan Gregg, for resources): Utilization, Saturation, Errors — per resource.
  • RED (Tom Wilkie, for request-driven services): Rate, Errors, Duration — essentially golden signals minus saturation.

06Toil

Definition

Toil is operational work that is manual, repetitive, automatable, tactical, devoid of enduring value, and scales linearly (O(n)) with service size. It's not just "work I dislike" — overhead like email and meetings isn't toil. Restarting a stuck server by hand every week is toil.

The <50% rule

Google caps toil at 50% of each SRE's time. The other half must go to engineering — automation, software, and durable reliability improvements that reduce future toil. If a team persistently exceeds 50%, that's a management signal to shed load back to the dev team or invest in automation.

Why cap it

  • Toil scales with the service → linear headcount growth → doesn't scale.
  • It's demoralizing and causes attrition/burnout.
  • It crowds out the engineering that would eliminate it (vicious cycle).
  • Career stagnation — no skill growth from repetitive ops.
⚠ Trap "Automate everything!" — no. Automate when the cost to automate < the cumulative cost of the toil (the classic "is it worth the time?" calculation over the automation's lifetime). Some rare, high-judgment tasks are cheaper left manual. And beware automating a broken process — you just make bad things happen faster.

07Incident management

Incident Command System (ICS) roles

Borrowed from emergency services. Separating roles prevents the "everyone debugging, no one coordinating" failure mode.

Core incident roles
RoleOwnsDoes NOT do
Incident Commander (IC)Overall coordination, delegation, decisions, keeping the response on track. The single point of authority.Should not get hands-dirty debugging — coordinates, doesn't fix.
Operations / Ops LeadThe only role that touches the system — applies fixes, runs diagnostics, mitigations.Doesn't manage comms or external updates.
Communications (Comms)Stakeholder updates, status page, exec briefings, customer messaging. The public face.Doesn't debug or command.
Planning (optional)Tracks long-running state, handoffs, filing the postmortem, logistics for extended incidents.—
Best practices
  • Clear handoffs — the IC role transfers explicitly ("you are now IC"), never ambiguously, especially across follow-the-sun shifts.
  • Live incident doc — a shared, timestamped log of what's known, tried, and decided. Becomes the postmortem's raw material.
  • Prioritize mitigation over root cause — stop the bleeding first (roll back, drain, failover), diagnose later.
  • Declare early — it's cheaper to spin up incident response and stand down than to escalate late.

The reliability metrics — MTTx

MetricMeaningFormula / note
MTTDMean Time To Detect — outage start → alert fires.Improve with better monitoring & burn-rate alerts.
MTTAMean Time To Acknowledge — alert → human responds.Pager hygiene, on-call readiness.
MTTRMean Time To Repair/Recover/Resolve — detection → service restored.Improve with runbooks, rollback, automation.

⚠ Trap MTTR is a noisy, skewed metric — a few catastrophic incidents dominate the mean, and "mean" over a handful of incidents is statistically weak. Interviewers reward candidates who say they'd look at the distribution, use medians, and treat MTTR as a directional trend, not a precise KPI. Reducing MTTR (fast rollback/mitigation) is often more valuable than chasing MTBF (preventing every failure).

08On-call & alert fatigue

Rotation design

  • Follow-the-sun — hand off between geographically distributed teams so nobody works nights. Requires clean handoffs.
  • Single-site alternative: weekly primary + secondary (backup/escalation) rotation.
  • Google guidance: cap on-call load so an engineer handles ≤ 2 incidents per on-call shift — more means the team is too small or too noisy.
  • Compensation — on-call is paid (time-off or cash), which also disincentivizes overloading a rotation.

Pager hygiene / fighting alert fatigue

  • Every page must be actionable and urgent. If it doesn't need human action now, it's a ticket, not a page.
  • Alert on symptoms (user impact), not causes — one "checkout failing" page beats 40 "CPU high on host-x" pages.
  • Delete/tune alerts that fire without action ("no-op pages").
  • Every page should map to a runbook/playbook entry.
  • Track page volume as a health metric; a noisy rotation causes desensitization — the real page gets ignored among the noise.
⚠ Trap Alert fatigue is a reliability risk, not just a morale one: when responders are numbed by hundreds of low-value alerts, they miss or slow-ack the one that matters, directly inflating MTTD/MTTA. "More alerts = safer" is backwards.

09Blameless postmortems

Blameless means the write-up focuses on systems and contributing factors, never on punishing individuals. The premise: people act rationally given the information, tooling, and incentives they had — so if a human "caused" it, the real fix is the system that allowed one human to. Blame drives incidents underground; blamelessness surfaces them so they get fixed.

Structure of a good postmortem

SectionContents
SummaryOne-paragraph what/impact/duration.
ImpactUsers affected, requests/revenue lost, SLO/error-budget burned.
TimelineTimestamped sequence: detection → escalation → mitigation → resolution.
Root cause & contributing factorsThe causal chain — usually multiple factors, not one.
What went well / what went poorlyHonest assessment of the response, not just the failure.
DetectionHow it was found; could it have been found sooner (MTTD)?
Action itemsSpecific, owned, tracked, prioritized follow-ups.
Lessons learnedReusable insight for other teams.
Root cause vs contributing factors

Modern SRE rejects the idea of a single root cause. Complex systems fail from a combination of contributing factors (Swiss-cheese model — several latent holes lining up). "Human error" is never a root cause — it's a symptom pointing at a missing guardrail. Use techniques like the 5 Whys, but keep asking past the first human in the chain.

Action items that actually help
  • SMART & owned — each has a named owner and a ticket, or it won't happen.
  • Prefer systemic fixes (add a guardrail, an automated check, a canary) over "be more careful" or "add training."
  • Track completion — a postmortem whose action items are never done is theatre.
  • Trigger criteria — write a postmortem for any incident that crosses a threshold (user-visible, SLO-impacting, took manual intervention), even if narrowly averted.
⚠ Trap "Blameless" is not "no accountability." The team is still accountable for fixing the system and completing action items. Blameless removes fear of personal punishment so facts flow freely — it does not remove responsibility for improvement.

11Capacity, chaos & progressive delivery

Capacity planning

  • Forecast demand (organic growth + launches/events) and provision ahead of it, with headroom for N+1 failures.
  • Drive it with load tests to find the real per-instance ceiling, not a guess.
  • Plan for both organic (steady growth) and inorganic (launch spike) demand.
  • Autoscaling handles short-term; capacity planning handles the long-term lead-time resources (quota, hardware, regions).

Chaos engineering / game days

  • Deliberately inject failure (kill nodes, add latency, drop a zone) to verify resilience holds before reality tests it.
  • Form a hypothesis, blast-radius-limit it, run in prod-like (ideally prod) conditions, observe, fix gaps.
  • Game days — scheduled, cross-team exercises rehearsing incident response & runbooks.
  • Netflix's Chaos Monkey is the canonical origin.

Progressive delivery (deployment strategies)

StrategyHowTrade-off
CanaryRoute a small % of traffic to the new version; watch SLIs; ramp up if healthy, roll back if not.Best risk/cost balance; needs good metrics & automated analysis to judge the canary.
Blue-greenTwo full environments; flip all traffic from blue (old) to green (new) at once; instant rollback by flipping back.Fast rollback but 2× infra cost; DB schema compatibility is the hard part.
RollingReplace instances batch by batch.No extra infra, but mixed versions live simultaneously; slower rollback.
Feature flagsDecouple deploy from release; toggle features on/off (or per-cohort) without redeploying. Kill switch for bad features.Flag debt; must be cleaned up. Enables dark launches & A/B.
Key distinction Deploy ≠ release. Deployment puts code on servers; release exposes it to users. Feature flags and canaries let you deploy continuously while releasing carefully — the foundation of low-risk, high-velocity delivery.

12Release engineering & change management

  • Most incidents are caused by change (deploys, config, flag flips). So change management is reliability's biggest lever.
  • Reproducible, hermetic builds — same source + same tools ⇒ identical artifact; pinned dependencies; build from version control, not a laptop.
  • Progressive rollouts + fast rollback — every change must have a tested, one-click undo. "Roll back first, diagnose later."
  • Self-service & automated — release tooling owned by SRE but usable by devs, gated by automated tests, canary analysis, and error-budget policy.
  • Config as code — configuration changes go through the same review/test/rollout as code, because they cause just as many outages.
Toil vs engineering — the balance

The whole SRE operating model is a lever: error budgets govern how much change velocity reliability can afford; the 50% toil cap guarantees SREs keep enough engineering time to automate the toil away; postmortems convert incidents into systemic fixes. Take away any one and the model degrades — unbounded toil starves the automation that would end the toil; no error budget makes reliability a subjective fight; no blameless culture and incidents hide. Interviewers want you to see these as a system, not a checklist.

13Rapid-fire Q&A

Q&A

Q: What's the difference between SRE and DevOps?

DevOps is a culture/philosophy (break down dev/ops silos, automate, shared ownership). SRE is a specific implementation of those principles with prescriptive practices — SLOs, error budgets, toil caps, blameless postmortems. "SRE implements DevOps" (class SRE implements interface DevOps).

Q&A

Q: Your service is at 99.99% but the SLO is 99.9%. What do you do?

You're over-delivering and hoarding error budget. Options: ship faster / take more risk, run chaos experiments, do risky migrations, or loosen the SLO. Over-reliability is wasted engineering that could be feature velocity. Unspent budget is a problem, not a trophy.

Q&A

Q: How do you set an SLO for a brand-new service?

Start from user expectations and dependencies (your SLO can't exceed the product of your critical dependencies' SLOs). Measure current performance for a few weeks to get a realistic baseline, pick an achievable-but-meaningful target, set the internal SLO tighter than any SLA, and iterate. Don't invent "four nines" out of thin air.

Q&A

Q: A dependency you call has a 99.9% SLO and you call it on every request. What's your ceiling?

Your availability can't exceed it on that path — roughly 99.9% at best, and if you have several such serial hard dependencies you multiply them (0.999 × 0.999 × …), so more dependencies = lower ceiling. Fixes: make the dependency non-critical (cache, degrade gracefully, async), add redundancy, or reduce fan-out.

Q&A

Q: 99.9% monthly — how much downtime is that? Derive it.

30-day month = 43,200 min. Budget = 0.1% = 43,200 × 0.001 = 43.2 min = 43m 12s. Each extra nine ÷10: 99.99% ≈ 4m 19s, 99.95% ≈ 21m 36s.

Q&A

Q: Why prefer symptom-based alerting over cause-based?

Symptoms (user-visible: high error rate, high latency) are what actually matter and are stable across implementation changes; causes (high CPU, full disk) are numerous, noisy, and may not affect users. Page on symptoms, use causes for diagnosis. Fewer, more meaningful pages ⇒ less alert fatigue.

Q&A

Q: How do you reduce MTTR?

Faster detection (good monitoring, burn-rate alerts), well-rehearsed runbooks/playbooks, one-click rollback and feature-flag kill switches, clear incident roles (IC/Ops/Comms), automation of common mitigations, and game-day practice. Mitigate first, root-cause later.

Q&A

Q: When would you NOT automate something?

When automation cost > lifetime toil cost, when the task is rare and needs human judgment, when the underlying process is still changing/broken (automate a broken process and you scale the breakage), or when a bad automation's blast radius is unacceptable. Automation itself needs guardrails, tests, and a manual override.

Q&A

Q: What makes a postmortem "blameless" and why does it matter?

It focuses on systemic contributing factors and fixes, not on punishing the person who pushed the button. It matters because fear suppresses honest disclosure — if people are blamed, incidents get hidden and recur. Blamelessness maximizes learning while still holding the team accountable for completing fixes.

Q&A

Q: Design an alert for a 99.9% SLO. Walk me through it.

Multi-window, multi-burn-rate. A fast-burn page at 14.4× over a 1h window (confirmed by a 5m short window) — catches catastrophic burn (2% of budget/hr, breach in ~2 days). A slower 6× over 6h page, plus 3×/1-day and 1×/3-day tickets for slow leaks. Requiring both a long and short window gives good precision (few false pages) and fast reset when it clears.

Q&A

Q: What's the difference between resilience and reliability?

Reliability = the system performs correctly over time (measured by SLIs/uptime). Resilience = the system's ability to absorb and recover from faults and stress (redundancy, degradation, circuit breakers). Resilience is a means; reliability is the outcome you measure.
